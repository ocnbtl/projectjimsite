# Phase 1 research and website strategy

Research date: July 11, 2026
Business: Masonry Color Corrections LLC
Market: Greater Cincinnati, Ohio
Internal project: Project Jimsite

## Executive direction

Masonry Color Corrections should be positioned as the Cincinnati-area specialist who makes mismatched masonry look intentional and completed repairs look as though they have always belonged.

The strongest initial message is not a vague promise to “transform” masonry. It is a specific outcome:

> Precision color matching for brick, mortar, stone, repairs, and additions.

Draft positioning line:

> Match what changed. Preserve what makes it masonry.

Draft conversion promise:

> Send photos of the mismatch and get a clear recommendation for the next step.

These are strategic drafts, not approved public claims.

The first release should prioritize three things:

1. Show real, labeled before-and-after evidence.
2. Explain why breathable masonry stain is different from surface paint without making unsupported product or durability claims.
3. Let a visitor call or send project photos within one or two actions on any device.

## Evidence standard used in this document

- **Verified** means the information was visible on the company’s current Facebook page or another directly inspected source.
- **Corroborating lead** means a separate source appears related but the relationship has not been confirmed by the client.
- **Hypothesis** means it is strategically plausible but must not be published as fact without client confirmation.

## Competitive conclusion

The clearest market gap is a specialist website that combines:

- Masonry Cosmetics’ educational rigor.
- Mid-Valley’s direct Cincinnati and service-intent coverage.
- Level Up’s “repairs should disappear” emotional outcome.
- Better project proof, visual design, accessibility, and conversion quality than any of the local sites inspected.

Masonry Color Corrections should not try to look like the biggest general masonry contractor. It should look like the most precise specialist for color problems that ordinary repair or replacement does not solve well.

## Homepage conversion blueprint

1. **Utility strip:** Cincinnati-area service cue and tap-to-call number.
2. **Header:** restrained logo, compact navigation, high-contrast estimate button.
3. **Hero:** outcome-led headline, one clear explanation, call and photo-estimate actions, and a genuine before/after comparison.
4. **Immediate trust row:** confirmed experience, insured/certified status, service area, response time, or review rating. Do not show unverified badges.
5. **Problem recognition:** repaired brick, additions, mortar mismatch, fireplace/stone, fading, and whole-surface changes. Use only confirmed services.
6. **Signature comparison:** large labeled case with the customer’s problem, correction approach, and result.
7. **Service pathways:** color matching, broader staining, and other confirmed selective correction work.
8. **Stain versus paint:** simple, evidence-based explanation tied to the company’s actual system.
9. **Process:** assess, test/sample, prepare, color-correct, inspect. Replace this draft with the real workflow.
10. **Project grid:** editorial selection of before/after cases, not an unlabeled image wall.
11. **FAQ:** candidacy, color selection, longevity/warranty, weather, timeline, painted brick, and estimate requirements.
12. **Service-area section:** one useful regional overview with real coverage limits.
13. **Estimate CTA:** phone option plus photo-first form.
14. **Footer:** canonical NAP, hours, service links, privacy, and social links.

## Design direction

### What the supplied bee_ui.ux references consistently do

- Place the interface inside a confident rounded frame rather than letting every element float independently.
- Use one dominant image or content field with smaller supporting modules.
- Combine strict grid alignment with one controlled asymmetric move.
- Use large, short headlines, small utility labels, restrained pills, and ample negative space.
- Layer interface-scale imagery over a larger atmospheric background.
- Crop media boldly and let one edge or card partially leave the frame.
- Use a limited palette and strong tonal contrast.
- Treat desktop and mobile as composed layouts, not simple scaled versions.
- Show polished wireframe-to-result thinking: hierarchy is settled before decoration.

### Original masonry adaptation

Working design concept: **Precision in every joint.**

- Use a limestone or warm mortar background, charcoal text, fired-clay red, weathered umber, and a restrained mineral accent.
- Let the main content frame echo a masonry opening or inset panel, with moderate radii rather than software-style bubbles.
- Build subtle grid rhythm from brick bond proportions without using a literal brick pattern behind every section.
- Make real masonry texture and tonal variation the premium material. Avoid stock images of generic masons when project work is available.
- Use a hero comparison as the dominant visual field, with small evidence chips for material, project type, and location.
- Use an editorial work grid: one large transformation, one narrow detail crop, and one process/sample card.
- Pair a confident grotesk headline face with a highly readable sans-serif body. Final fonts will be tested for performance and licensing.
- Use motion only to clarify comparison or sequencing. Respect reduced-motion preferences.

### Avoid

- Fake browser chrome copied from the references.
- A dark “luxury contractor” template with gold gradients.
- Excessive pill buttons, glassmorphism, floating cards, or dashboard-like UI.
- Text baked into project images.
- Unlabeled before/after sliders that are inaccessible by keyboard.
- Decorative texture that lowers contrast or makes the business feel dusty or old-fashioned.
- Copy that sounds like a national manufacturer if this is a local craft specialist.

### Accessibility and performance requirements

- WCAG 2.2 AA color contrast and visible focus states.
- Semantic heading order and landmarks.
- Large tap targets and tap-to-call links.
- Keyboard-operable comparison controls with noninteractive fallback images.
- Reduced-motion handling.
- Error summaries, field-level help, and status announcements for the estimate form.
- Project image alt text based on what changed, not keyword stuffing.
- Responsive image formats, defined dimensions, lazy loading below the fold, and a disciplined font budget.

## Recommended content proof standard

Every featured project should answer:

- What looked wrong?
- What material was involved?
- Why replacement alone did not solve the visual problem.
- What was corrected or stained?
- How was the target color established?
- What limitations or surface conditions mattered?
- What does the finished work look like from normal viewing distance?
- Where was the project in general terms?
- Does the client authorize publication?

This turns a photo gallery into conversion evidence and creates original local-search content.

## Recommended implementation direction

This can remain a fast, mostly static marketing site with modular content:

- Next.js with TypeScript and a restrained component system.
- Server-rendered routes and optimized responsive images.
- Case studies stored as structured local content initially; add a CMS only if the client truly needs independent editing.
- Secure server-side estimate endpoint with spam protection, file validation, delivery logging, and a privacy-aware upload strategy.
- Vercel or equivalent managed deployment, with preview deployments for review.
- Analytics limited to the decisions the business will use: calls, estimate starts, successful estimate submissions, and major CTA source.

The final stack should be locked after domain, hosting, form delivery, photo upload, editing, and analytics requirements are confirmed.
